1. Wafer Manufacturing

First Things First!

What's the #1 requirement for wafer manufacturing?

Ultra-pure silicon! (99.99% pure or better)

Why is ultra-purity non-negotiable?

For reliability, high-performance and precise doping.

Even nanoscale impurities can make a wafer unusable!

How to make electronic-grade silicon (EGS) from sand?

Through some chemical magic!

EGS Preparation

Transforming quartzite (sand) into metallurgical-grade silicon (98% pure)

\[\textbf{SiC(s)} + \textbf{SiO}_2\textbf{(s)} \xrightarrow{} \textbf{Si(s)} + \textbf{SiO(g)} + \textbf{CO(g)}\]

Impurity removal (for 99.99% purity) through volatile trichlorosilane formation and fractional distillation

\[\textbf{Si(s)} + 3\textbf{HCl(g)} \xrightarrow{300^\circ\text{C}} \textbf{SiHCl}_3\textbf{(g)} + \textbf{H}_2\textbf{(g)}\]

Hydrogen reduction reaction for preparing EGS

\[\textbf{SiHCl}_3\textbf{(g)} + \textbf{H}_2\textbf{(g)} \xrightarrow{} \textbf{Si(s)} + 3\textbf{HCl(g)}\]

Single-Crystal Silicon

What is single-crystal (monocrystalline) silicon?

A perfect, unbroken atomic lattice where all silicon atoms are arranged in a continuous, repeating 3D pattern

Imagine a highway where all lanes run perfectly straight (single-crystal) vs. one with constant intersections (polycrystalline) – electrons are the cars!

Crystal Growth

Why do we need single-crystal silicon?

Single-crystal silicon provides a perfectly uniform atomic structure without grain boundaries, enabling predictable electron flow and consistent electrical properties across the entire wafer.

How is EGS converted into single-crystal ingots?

Through crystal growth methods like the Czochralski (CZ) or Float Zone (FZ) processes.

What do we add to silicon to control its conductivity?

Tiny dopant amounts of boron (for p-type) or phosphorus (for n-type).

Czochralski Method

What’s the point of a seed?

To give the molten silicon a perfect starting pattern to copy as it freezes.

Why is argon gas pumped into the furnace?

To protect the molten silicon from air contamination while cleaning up waste gases.

Wafer Slicing & Testing

How are single-crystal ingots transformed into thin wafers?

Using diamond wire saws that cut with hair-thin precision (less than 0.5mm loss per wafer)

What quality checks ensure wafers meet chip fabrication standards?

X-ray crystal scans, laser defect mapping, and nanoscale thickness and resistivity tests – a full silicon health screening.

2. Oxidation

Why oxidize?

Silicon $\textbf{(Si)}$ has dangling bonds and surface defects that can trap charges.

$\textbf{SiO}_2$ passivates the $\textbf{Si}$ surface by bonding with dangling $\textbf{Si}$ bonds.

Oxidation acts as an investment!

It facilitates making masks for doping and etching, which is critical to the fabrication process.

$\textbf{Si}$ is fragile and may break during handling.

$\textbf{SiO}_2$ is a shield against environmental contaminants and damages.

Growth Methods

Thermal Oxidation

The most widely used method

Involves heating silicon wafers in an oxygen-rich or steam environment

$\textbf{I.}$ Dry Oxidation ($\textbf{O}_2$ Environment)

$\textbf{Si} + \textbf{O}_2\xrightarrow{} \textbf{SiO}_2$

High-quality, dense $\textbf{SiO}_2$

Ideal for thin gate oxides

Slow growth

$\textbf{II.}$ Wet Oxidation ($\textbf{H}_2\textbf{O}$ / Steam Environment)

$\textbf{Si} + \textbf{2H}_2\textbf{O}\xrightarrow{} \textbf{SiO}_2 + \textbf{2H}_2$

Less density and more defects

Thicker oxides

3 to 5 times faster

Other Growth Methods

Name Principle Advantage
High-Pressure Oxidation Raising Pressure Faster growth at lower temperatures
Rapid Thermal Oxidation Rapid Thermal Processing Ultrathin and High-Quality
Plasma-Enhanced Oxidation Plasma at Low Temperatures Low Thermal Budget

Growth Kinetics: The Deal-Grove Model

$$ \textbf{t}_{ox}^2 + \textbf{At}_{ox} = \textbf{B(t + τ)} $$

$\textbf{t}_{ox} = $ oxide thickness

$\textbf{A} , \textbf{B} = $ rate constants

$\textbf{τ} = $ initial native oxide correction

3. Photolithography/Patterning

Optical Lithography

How does optical lithography create chip patterns?

It uses ultraviolet light (UV) to "print" tiny circuit patterns onto silicon.

Why is UV light (wavelengths between 0.2–0.4 µm) used in optical lithography?

Short wavelength of UV enables printing nanoscale circuit patterns—the smaller the light, the tinier the features!

At what particle size do contamination risks become critical for wafers?

Even 0.1µm particles can cause defects—that's why we use clean rooms (ISO Class 1), which filter air to near-zero particles.

Exposure Tools

What’s the "magic wand" that prints patterns onto silicon?

Lithographic exposure tools—machines that shine UV light through masks!

What are the parameters for a good exposure tool?

Resolution, registration and throughput.

DUV vs. EUV?

DUV (Deep UV) → Uses 193nm light: Workhorse for most modern chips (e.g. smartphones), but hits a wall at ~10nm features.

EUV (Extreme UV) → Uses 13.5nm light: Enables atomic-scale 3nm chips, but demands a vacuum chamber which has significantly higher costs.

Masks and Photoresist

What roles do masks and photoresist play?

Photomask → The stencil: A glass plate with opaque (chrome) patterns that block/allow light.

Photoresist → The light-sensitive canvas: A liquid coating that hardens or dissolves when exposed to UV light.

4. Etching and Doping

How Etching Helps

Etching selectively removes layers to create patterns defined by lithography.

It's like carving wood through the holes of a stencil—the stencil (resist) protects some areas while the knife (etchant) removes material elsewhere.

$\textbf{SiO}_2$ would block dopants from reaching the silicon substrate.

Remove $\textbf{SiO}_2$ only from regions where the p-n junction should form.

Dopants would spread uniformly, creating a poorly controlled junction.

Etching prevents unwanted doping in masked regions.

Etching Methods

Wet Etching Dry Etching
Uses liquid chemicals (e.g. $\text{HF}$) Uses reactive ions in a plasma (e.g. $\text{F}^*$)
Isotropic (rounded profiles) Anisotropic (vertical sidewalls)
Simple, low-cost, high selectivity High precision (sub-10 nm features)
Poor anisotropy (limits miniaturization) Expensive equipment, plasma-induced damage

What's doping for?

Pure silicon is a poor conductor because it has very few free charge carriers.

Doping introduces impurities to increases conductivity by orders of magnitude.

Diffusion

The wafer is exposed to a dopant gas (e.g., \( \text{B}_2\text{H}_6 \) for p-type or \( \text{POCl}_3 \) for n-type) at high temperatures (800–1200°C).

Dopant atoms adsorb onto the silicon surface and begin diffusing into the lattice.

The wafer is heated further to drive dopants deeper into the silicon.

Ion Implantation

Ion implantation has largely replaced thermal diffusion in modern semiconductor manufacturing.

Due to its precision, scalability, and compatibility

Dopant ions (e.g., \( \text{B}^+ \), \( \text{P}^+ \), \( \text{As}^+ \)) are accelerated by a 1–500 keV electric field.

The beam is scanned across the wafer.

Ions penetrate the silicon lattice, stopping at a depth determined by their energy and mass.

Rapid Thermal Annealing (RTA) at 900–1100°C repairs lattice damage and activates dopants.

Parameter Diffusion Ion Implantation
Control Poor ($\text{µm}$-scale) Excellent ($\text{nm}$-scale)
Thermal Budget High (long, high-temp steps) Low (RTA for seconds)
Damage None Lattice defects (repaired by anneal)
Cost Low High
