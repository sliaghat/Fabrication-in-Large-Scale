1. Wafer Manufacturing

First Things First!

What's the #1 requirement for wafer manufacturing?

Ultra-pure silicon! (99.99% pure or better)

Why is ultra-purity non-negotiable?

For reliability, high-performance and precise doping.

Even nanoscale impurities can make a wafer unusable!

How to make electronic-grade silicon (EGS) from sand?

Through some chemical magic!

EGS Preparation

Transforming quartzite (sand) into metallurgical-grade silicon (98% pure)

\[\textbf{SiC(s)} + \textbf{SiO}_2\textbf{(s)} \xrightarrow{} \textbf{Si(s)} + \textbf{SiO(g)} + \textbf{CO(g)}\]

Impurity removal (for 99.99% purity) through volatile trichlorosilane formation and fractional distillation

\[\textbf{Si(s)} + 3\textbf{HCl(g)} \xrightarrow{300^\circ\text{C}} \textbf{SiHCl}_3\textbf{(g)} + \textbf{H}_2\textbf{(g)}\]

Hydrogen reduction reaction for preparing EGS

\[\textbf{SiHCl}_3\textbf{(g)} + \textbf{H}_2\textbf{(g)} \xrightarrow{} \textbf{Si(s)} + 3\textbf{HCl(g)}\]

Single-Crystal Silicon

What is single-crystal (monocrystalline) silicon?

A perfect, unbroken atomic lattice where all silicon atoms are arranged in a continuous, repeating 3D pattern

Imagine a highway where all lanes run perfectly straight (single-crystal) vs. one with constant intersections (polycrystalline) – electrons are the cars!

Crystal Growth

Why do we need single-crystal silicon?

Single-crystal silicon provides a perfectly uniform atomic structure without grain boundaries, enabling predictable electron flow and consistent electrical properties across the entire wafer.

How is EGS converted into single-crystal ingots?

Through crystal growth methods like the Czochralski (CZ) or Float Zone (FZ) processes.

Czochralski Method

What’s the point of a seed?

To give the molten silicon a perfect starting pattern to copy as it freezes.

Why is argon gas pumped into the furnace?

To protect the molten silicon from air contamination while cleaning up waste gases.

2. Oxidation

Why oxidize?

Silicon $\textbf{(Si)}$ has dangling bonds and surface defects that can trap charges.

$\textbf{SiO}_2$ passivates the $\textbf{Si}$ surface by bonding with dangling $\textbf{Si}$ bonds.

Oxidation acts as an investment!

It facilitates making masks for doping and etching, which is critical to the fabrication process.

$\textbf{Si}$ is fragile and may break during handling.

$\textbf{SiO}_2$ is a shield against environmental contaminants and damages.

Growth Methods

Thermal Oxidation

The most widely used method

Involves heating silicon wafers in an oxygen-rich or steam environment

$\textbf{I.}$ Dry Oxidation ($\textbf{O}_2$ Environment)

$\textbf{Si} + \textbf{O}_2\xrightarrow{} \textbf{SiO}_2$

High-quality, dense $\textbf{SiO}_2$

Ideal for thin gate oxides

Slow growth

$\textbf{II.}$ Wet Oxidation ($\textbf{H}_2\textbf{O}$ / Steam Environment)

$\textbf{Si} + \textbf{2H}_2\textbf{O}\xrightarrow{} \textbf{SiO}_2 + \textbf{2H}_2$

Less density and more defects

Thicker oxides

3 to 5 times faster

Other Growth Methods

Name Principle Advantage
High-Pressure Oxidation Raising Pressure Faster growth at lower temperatures
Rapid Thermal Oxidation Rapid Thermal Processing Ultrathin and High-Quality
Plasma-Enhanced Oxidation Plasma at Low Temperatures Low Thermal Budget

Growth Kinetics: The Deal-Grove Model

$$ \textbf{t}_{ox}^2 + \textbf{At}_{ox} = \textbf{B(t + τ)} $$

$\textbf{t}_{ox} = $ oxide thickness

$\textbf{A} , \textbf{B} = $ rate constants

$\textbf{τ} = $ initial native oxide correction
